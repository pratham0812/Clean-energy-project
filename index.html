Clean and Affordable Energy

Clean and affordable energy is a major sustainable development goal set by the UN. It aims to promote energy production methods that do not harm the environment. Currently, 80% of our energy needs are met by traditional means such as fossil fuels. Since clean energy is still not affordable for everyone and cannot yet meet all our energy demands, significant investments are being made in clean energy to ensure future energy security. Renewable energy solutions are becoming cheaper, more reliable, and more efficient every day. Our reliance on fossil fuels is unsustainable and harmful to the planet, necessitating a change in how we produce and consume energy. Rapid implementation of these new energy solutions is crucial to counter climate change, one of the biggest threats to our survival

Affordable energy is a critical issue that needs to be tackled to ensure a quality life for all. Access to affordable energy is essential for economic development, education, healthcare, and overall well-being. However, many regions still face significant challenges in providing affordable energy to their populations. The slow pace of progress towards universal access to electricity (SDG indicator 7.1.1) reflects the ongoing struggle. Despite advancements, the share of the global population with access to electricity only increased from 78 percent in 2000 to 91 percent in 2021. Projections indicate a marginal rise to 92 percent by 2030, highlighting the urgent need for accelerated efforts.

South Asia has made remarkable progress, with electricity access increasing from 58 percent in 2000 to 99 percent in 2021. However, Sub-Saharan Africa continues to lag, with access rising from 26 percent to 51 percent during the same period. In 2021, half of the population in Sub-Saharan Africa still lacked electricity, and access would need to more than double by 2030 to meet SDG target 7.1. The urban-rural gap exacerbates the issue, with rural areas containing 80 percent of the world's population without electricity. In Sub-Saharan Africa, the disparity is stark: 30 percent of rural residents had electricity in 2021 compared to 81 percent in urban areas. Addressing this gap is crucial for achieving equitable energy access and improving the quality of life for all, particularly in low- and lower-middle-income countries.

Sustainable Energy Targets

Target 7.1: Universal Access to Modern Energy

By 2030, ensure universal access to affordable, reliable, and modern energy services.

Target 7.2: Increase Global Percentage of Renewable Energy

By 2030, substantially increase the share of renewable energy in the global energy mix.

Target 7.3: Double the Improvement in Energy Efficiency

By 2030, double the global rate of improvement in energy efficiency.

Target 7.4: Promote Access to Research, Technology, and Investments in Clean Energy

By 2030, enhance international cooperation to facilitate access to clean energy research and technology, including renewable energy, energy efficiency, and advanced cleaner fossil-fuel technology, and promote investment in energy infrastructure and clean energy technology.

Target 7.5: Expand and Upgrade Energy Services for Developing Countries

By 2030, expand infrastructure and upgrade technology for supplying modern and sustainable energy services for all in developing countries, especially least developed countries, small island developing states, and landlocked developing countries, in accordance with their respective programs of support.

Current Problems

Energy Storage

  • Efficient energy storage systems are crucial for renewable energy sources (like solar and wind) that fluctuate.
  • Batteries (such as lithium-ion) store excess energy for use during low production periods.
  • Innovations in grid-scale storage, like pumped hydro and flow batteries, enhance reliability.

Energy Poverty

  • Over a billion people lack access to electricity globally, leading to energy poverty.
  • Solutions include decentralized mini-grids, off-grid solar systems, and community-based projects.
  • Addressing energy poverty contributes to economic development and social equity.

Policy and Regulation

  • Clear policies and incentives drive investment in clean energy.
  • Regulatory frameworks impact adoption rates and technology deployment.
  • Balancing environmental goals with affordability is essential.

Decarbonization Challenges

  • Transitioning from fossil fuels to renewables requires systemic changes.
  • Decarbonizing sectors like transportation, industry, and heating remains a challenge.

Next-Generation Renewables

  • Research into next-generation solar cells (e.g., perovskite solar cells) and wind turbine designs aim to increase efficiency and reduce costs.

Financial Mechanisms

  • Mobilizing private and public capital is essential for scaling clean energy projects. Green bonds, climate funds, and public-private partnerships play a vital role.
  • Financial instruments like insurance and guarantees help mitigate risks associated with renewable energy investments, encouraging more stakeholders to participate.

Global Collaboration

  • International agreements like the Paris Agreement provide a framework for global cooperation on climate action and sustainable energy.
  • Platforms for sharing best practices, technology transfer, and capacity building are crucial for accelerating clean energy adoption worldwide.
  • Initiatives like the International Solar Alliance (ISA) and Sustainable Energy for All (SEforALL) promote collaborative efforts to achieve universal energy access and sustainability.

Government Initiatives

Renewable Energy Incentives

  • Subsidies and Tax Credits: Many governments offer financial incentives to encourage the adoption of renewable energy sources like solar, wind, and hydropower. These incentives may include tax credits, rebates, and grants for both individuals and businesses.
  • Feed-in Tariffs: These programs guarantee a fixed premium price for renewable energy fed into the grid, encouraging investment in renewable energy infrastructure.

Energy Efficiency Programs

  • Energy Efficiency Standards: Governments set efficiency standards for appliances, vehicles, and buildings to reduce energy consumption. Programs like ENERGY STAR in the U.S. certify energy-efficient products.
  • Retrofitting and Weatherization: Programs that provide funding or incentives for retrofitting buildings with energy-efficient technologies and weatherization measures to reduce energy waste.

Research and Development

  • Funding for Innovation: Governments invest in R&D for new energy technologies, including advanced solar panels, wind turbines, and energy storage solutions. This funding helps drive technological advancements and cost reductions.
  • Public-Private Partnerships: Collaborations between government agencies and private companies to develop and deploy innovative energy solutions.

Clean Energy Access Programs

  • Rural Electrification: Initiatives aimed at providing electricity to rural and underserved areas through renewable energy sources. These programs often involve installing solar panels, microgrids, and other off-grid solutions.
  • Affordability Measures: Subsidies and financial assistance programs to make clean energy more affordable for low-income households.

Policy and Regulatory Measures

  • Renewable Portfolio Standards (RPS): Mandates that a certain percentage of electricity must come from renewable sources. States or countries with RPS targets drive the market for renewable energy.
  • Carbon Pricing: Implementing carbon taxes or cap-and-trade systems to incentivize reductions in greenhouse gas emissions and make fossil fuel energy sources less attractive.

International Collaboration

  • Global Initiatives: Participation in international agreements like the Paris Agreement, which aims to combat climate change by reducing global greenhouse gas emissions and promoting renewable energy.
  • Bilateral and Multilateral Partnerships: Collaborations between countries to share technology, knowledge, and resources for clean energy development.

Education Awareness

  • Public Awareness Campaigns: Governments run campaigns to educate citizens about the benefits of clean energy and energy efficiency, encouraging behavior change and adoption of sustainable practices.
  • Training Programs: Providing training and certification programs for workers in the renewable energy sector to build a skilled workforce.

Clean Energy

Clean energy refers to energy generated from renewable, zero-emission sources that do not release pollutants into the atmosphere. These sources include solar, wind, hydroelectric, geothermal, and biomass energy. The deployment of clean energy technologies is essential for mitigating climate change, reducing air pollution, and enhancing energy security. Unlike fossil fuels, which emit greenhouse gases and other pollutants upon combustion, clean energy sources produce minimal to no emissions, rendering them a sustainable solution for the future. Technologies such as photovoltaic panels, wind turbines, and hydropower plants harness natural processes to generate electricity, offering an environmentally benign alternative to conventional energy sources.

The transition to clean energy is imperative for diminishing global reliance on fossil fuels, which currently dominate the energy sector. As of the latest data, clean energy sources account for approximately 29% of the global electricity supply, with hydropower being the predominant contributor, followed by wind and solar power. This proportion is progressively increasing as nations invest in renewable energy infrastructure and technological advancements render these sources more cost-effective. The shift to clean energy not only aids in combating climate change but also fosters sustainable economic growth, generates employment opportunities, and enhances public health by reducing pollution-related ailments.

Solar energy refers to the radiant light and heat from the sun that is harnessed using various technologies, primarily solar panels or photovoltaic cells, to generate electricity or provide heat. It is a renewable and sustainable source of energy, abundant in availability and environmentally friendly in its utilization. Solar energy plays a crucial role in reducing dependence on fossil fuels, mitigating greenhouse gas emissions, and addressing global energy challenges. It can be utilized both on a small scale, such as rooftop solar panels for individual homes, and on a large scale, through solar farms contributing to grid electricity. As technology advances and costs decrease, solar energy continues to grow as a key component of the global energy mix, offering clean and affordable power solutions for the future.

Wind energy utilizes the kinetic energy of wind to generate electricity through wind turbines. These turbines convert the wind's rotational energy into electrical power, which can be used locally or transmitted through power grids. Wind energy is a clean and renewable resource that contributes significantly to global electricity generation. It is abundant, widely distributed, and environmentally friendly compared to traditional energy sources. Wind farms, both onshore and offshore, are expanding globally, capitalizing on technological advancements to increase efficiency and reduce costs. Wind energy plays a vital role in reducing greenhouse gas emissions and enhancing energy security in many countries worldwide.

Tidal energy harnesses the natural ebb and flow of ocean tides to generate electricity. It involves capturing the kinetic energy of tidal currents using turbines placed underwater or along coastal areas. Tidal energy is a predictable and renewable resource, driven by gravitational forces from the moon and sun. It offers potential advantages in coastal regions with strong tidal currents, providing a consistent and stable source of electricity. While still in early stages of commercial development, tidal energy holds promise as a sustainable energy option with minimal environmental impact compared to conventional fossil fuels.

Hydropower harnesses the gravitational force of flowing water, typically from rivers or dams, to generate electricity. It involves channeling water through turbines, which spin generators to produce electricity. Hydropower is a mature and reliable renewable energy source, providing consistent base-load power and flexibility for energy storage through reservoirs. It is environmentally friendly in operation, with minimal greenhouse gas emissions compared to fossil fuels. Hydropower contributes significantly to global electricity supply, particularly in regions with abundant water resources. While concerns exist regarding ecological impacts and displacement of communities due to dam construction, technological advancements and sustainable practices continue to improve its efficiency and mitigate environmental effects.
